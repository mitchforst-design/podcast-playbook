Podcast Production

Podcast Playbook

The how to, what to, and why to of making a successful and sustainable podcast

This guide is designed for you to work through as you embark on launching a new podcast. It's meant to get you thinking about the best approaches and challenge you to consider things you might not have already. Ponder the questions and fill in the best you can. It is meant to be a difficult but collaborative process.

Chapter 1

What is the name of your show?

Naming your podcast is a critical step — it sets the tone and influences your audience's first impression. Consider carefully.

Your show name

12 factors to consider

  • Relevance to Content. Ensure the name reflects the podcast's theme or niche. It should give potential listeners an idea of what to expect.
  • Memorability. Choose a name that is easy to remember, spell, and pronounce. Search for your proposed name — you want it to come up first, not get lost in a sea of generic results.
  • SEO and Keywords. Incorporate relevant keywords in the title to improve discoverability. Think about what terms your potential audience might search for.
  • Domain Availability. Check if the podcast name has an available domain. Owning the domain helps with branding and online presence.
  • Originality. Make sure the name isn't already taken by another podcast or trademarked by another entity. This avoids legal issues and confusion.
  • Length. Keep it concise. Shorter names are generally easier to remember and fit better in social media handles and podcast directories.
  • Audience Appeal. Choose a name that resonates with your target audience. It should align with their interests and cultural context.
  • Emotional Impact. A name that evokes curiosity, humor, or an emotional response can be more engaging and create connection.
  • Social Media Availability. Ensure the name or a close variation is available as a handle on major platforms. Consistency strengthens your brand.
  • Acronyms and Abbreviations. If your name is long, consider how it will be abbreviated and whether that abbreviation is easy to use and remember.
  • Longevity. Choose a name that will stand the test of time and won't feel outdated as trends change.
  • Testing. Test the name with friends, family, or potential listeners to get feedback before making a final decision.

Chapter 2

Who is this show for?

Here's what podcasts do best: build connections. They don't broadcast to people — they speak directly to them.

Audio storytelling is powerful. It's in our DNA to pick up rhythms, patterns, inflections. These are all tools we have to play with.

Know your audience

The more you know about your audience, the better you can create a show for them. You can give them what you know they want, or counterprogram. You can make show choices that amplify shared values, speak directly to them, figure out how they listen, and build habits and your relationship with them.

Chapter 3

Is podcast cover art important?

The art on your podcast is shown to have a huge influence on whether people press play. Creating great podcast art is a crucial aspect of your show's branding.

15 considerations for great cover art

  • Clarity and Simplicity. Your art should be clear and easy to understand, even at thumbnail size. Avoid overcrowding the design with too many elements.
  • Reflects the Content. The design should visually represent the theme of your podcast. The imagery and style should give potential listeners a sense of what the show is about.
  • Brand Consistency. Ensure the art aligns with your overall branding — colors, fonts, and style used across your website, social media, and other materials.
  • Readable Text. If your art includes text, make sure it's legible at various sizes. Choose fonts that are clear and avoid overly ornate styles.
  • Color Choices. Use colors that stand out and reflect the mood and tone of your podcast. Search competitor podcasts — if it's a corporate space, you'll likely see a sea of blue. Use that to your advantage.
  • Scalability. Your artwork should look good at different sizes, from small thumbnails to larger display images. Test at various scales.
  • Platform Requirements. Follow the technical requirements of Apple Podcasts, Spotify, etc. Typically: square image, at least 1400×1400 pixels, JPEG or PNG format.
  • Uniqueness. Stand out from the crowd. Avoid generic designs or stock images that might be overused.
  • Emotional Appeal. Consider the emotions you want to evoke. Your art should resonate with the feelings you want to inspire in your listeners.
  • Consistency with Title. The podcast title and artwork should complement each other. The design should reinforce the podcast's name and theme.
  • Use of Icons or Symbols. Simple, relevant icons can be effective — but they should not clutter the design.
  • Professional Quality. If design isn't your strength, consider hiring a professional designer to create high-quality artwork that represents your podcast well.
  • Test with Audience. Get feedback from your target audience or peers on the design before finalizing it.
  • Timelessness. Create artwork that won't feel outdated quickly. Timeless designs will serve you better in the long run.
  • Flexibility for Updates. Consider how your design might need to evolve if your podcast grows or changes direction in the future.

Is your idea a Show, or a Series?

Some ideas make a great podcast. Some make a great podcast series. Still others make multiple seasons. Whether you dive deep into one story, tell one story per episode, or present a handful of stories each show will depend on a few things.

Why is this important?

You need to make sure you have a strong enough organizing principle to hold the show together, the right values to give it a defined personality for listeners to attach to, and enough material to sustain over time.

Chapter 7

Writing for Podcasts

There is no hero in podcast writing. The best scripts are built via constant collaboration between writers, producers, and show-runners.

Finding your show's voice

Much of the script will be host narration. Unless that host is you, you'll be channeling someone else's voice. Every host has their own vocal quirks, their own lexicon. Get to know your host — learn what their conversations sound like.

A brand is like the arena where your host is positioned. The host delivers their perspective from the vantage of the brand's landscape. Listen to how the host reads your scripts. Where did text and voice come together? Where did they miss each other?

Pro-tips

  • One thought per sentence is the golden rule (made to be broken — but only after you've mastered it)
  • If you add more than one comma to a sentence, break it into two sentences
  • Show don't tell — if you can paint a picture, a script will come alive in the theatre of the mind
  • Conversational, intimate tone at all times
  • No numbers unless absolutely necessary
  • Present tense — stories play out for the listener in real time
  • Active sentences: "She tosses the book onto the table" — not "The book was tossed onto the table by the woman"

Sign-Posting: No listener left behind

The ear does not remember the way the eye does. When we write for the ear, we need to repeat names and dates more often. We situate the listener constantly in time and place. Signposting is the connective tissue that moves a script from one clip to another, one guest to another, one story to the next.

Speed

Clear spoken language moves at about 150 words per minute; when we read to ourselves, we clip along at 230 words per minute. Because spoken words take longer to consume than written words, sluggish prose feels even more sluggish. The goal is to deliver both directness and conversational warmth simultaneously — a casual clarity.

Choosing clips

When we weave guest clips and host narration to tell a story, each has a role to play. Clips are for emotion and color, not process or statistics. Guests are there to tell us why something happened, how it felt, how it changed things. Write in and out of clips, anticipating first words and reflecting on last words.

Facts are recorded, but stories are remembered.

Chapter 8

Finding & Booking Guests

A quick introductory lesson in chase producing. On paper, the role of a chase producer is simple: locate guests and book them to appear on your show. In practice, it's a bit more complicated.

Questions to ask when considering a guest

  • How qualified is the individual to speak about the given topic?
  • What's their relationship to the story and how credible is their point of view?
  • Do they have any conflicts of interest that might bias their input?
  • How strong of a guest and/or speaker are they?
  • Are you representing an appropriate range of perspectives, opinions, and experiences?
  • What other voices are you featuring and how does this one complement them?
  • When is the guest available? Are they equipped to interview remotely?

Tips for finding guests

  • Social media is your friend. Most people are online. X.com (formerly Twitter) is an excellent resource for reaching out. Just keep your own profiles professional.
  • Don't be afraid to follow up. People are busy and miss emails. Give a guest a day or two, then a polite follow-up is perfectly appropriate.
  • Ask for help. Tap into your networks. Put the word out that you're looking to connect with someone who fits a certain description.
  • Reframe the ask. Requesting an interview isn't asking for a favor — you're offering a platform to share their expertise. Lead with why they'd want to participate.
  • It's OK to push. Getting someone to agree can take seconds, minutes, weeks, or even months. Don't give up at the first "no."
  • Ask for a stand-in. If your desired guest is unavailable, ask them to recommend someone else who might be a good fit.
  • Be sensitive and gauge expectations. Always be open and transparent about what you're looking for and how the guest's input will be used.

Sample outreach emails

After the interview

Chase producing isn't just about booking guests — it's about shepherding them through the process from start to finish. Once your guest has recorded their interview, always remember to thank them and provide instructions on where and how to listen to it. Invite them to share the episode on social media. Guests can act as wonderful ambassadors for your podcast.

Chapter 9

Interview Prep

Prepping a guest for interviewing is key to making them feel comfortable and getting the best performance out of them. We're building a relationship that starts with the first contact email.

How to get the best out of your interview

1

Prep yourself — research and focus

Have a clear idea of what type of voice you're looking for and what role the interviewee needs to play. Think about "casting" as carefully as one would in a movie. The clearer you are about what you need from the guest, the clearer they'll be too.

2

Prep your guest — the art of the pre-interview

Make sure the guest knows this is not "the interview." Be open and curious. Always end with: "Is there anything about this subject I should know that I haven't asked about?" Then assess whether to proceed to a formal recorded interview.

3

Prep the interviewer — focus statement and Q-line

Provide notes summarizing key things you want your guest to discuss, what they're like as a talker, and their role within the episode. Craft a focus statement before writing questions — it frames the entire interview.

The Focus Statement

A strong focus statement is about someone doing something, for a reason. It speaks to action AND motivation — and stakes and consequences. It comes before you write your questions.

💡 Example focus statements

First-person story: "Peter Parker patrols the streets at night wearing a spider mask because he has superpowers he feels he must use to keep his city safe — but he doesn't want to upset his Aunt May who worries about him."

Expert: "Jane Smith spends 8 hours a day poring over Edison's letters because, despite the inventor's reputation as a lone-wolf genius, Smith is convinced that Edison's greatest innovation was creating new, collaborative ways for teams to work together."

Chapter 10

Hosting for Podcasts

In the glamorous world of podcasting, hosts are most often recording themselves with a show producer on the line for coaching and continuity — from a professional studio, or the bedroom closet, or anything in between.

Before the recording session

  • Warm up with tongue twisters, singing, running scales — get the voicebox working
  • Stretch face and mouth muscles, open the throat, shoulders, back, chest
  • Voice projection exercises
  • Set mic levels and placement
  • Rehearse, rehearse, rehearse. You can read the script in your inside voice, but you can only rehearse it out loud. Table reads are a great time to rehearse without the tape rolling.

During the recording session

Podcasts are an intimate broadcast medium. They are not telling a story to 50 thousand people. They are telling a story to a single person, times 50 thousand. It's a one-to-one relationship.

  • Visualize a single individual you are speaking to
  • Keep a conversational tone — always speak to a person, not the page
  • Make it matter. Never be boring.
  • Speak across the mic, not directly into it (avoids popping)
  • Sit up straight, both feet planted firmly on the floor — opens up the airways
  • Smile, even if what you're saying is serious — it opens up the muscles in your face
  • Talk with your hands (just don't hit the mic)

Marking your script

Font size and margins are important. Your brain reads faster than your mouth can — you need to make sure it's not getting too far ahead. Think about organizing your pages so no content breaks over two pages. Use bold, underlines, italics, and color to hit the right emphasis.

💡 Script pro-tips

  • Note pronouncers on unusual words or names: e.g. Leah McLean = LAY-uh mih-KLANE
  • Using paper? Fold the top corner of each page separately so they don't stick together and are easy to flip
  • Wide margins help your brain and mouth track closer together
  • Group paragraphs together in scenes, and add breaks where you'd naturally pause

Who are you as host?

Hosts can play roles that run from confessor to narrator. Who is chosen as a host can reflect a lot about a show — who does it speak to? What is the subject matter? What is the tone? Hosts are literally the voice of the show. All of the content will flow through them. With branded content, whether you're on the brand's employee list or not, you are also the voice of their brand.

Chapter 11

Twice as Nice: Co-Hosting

All of the things that can make listening to a host great or awful is not just doubled, but amplified by the dynamic of host interaction.

Finding your roles

It helps the audience follow along if you can find "roles" for yourselves. Think about who you are in relation to each other and lean into that. This can be delineated through personality, expertise, or the content you bring. Some starting points:

  • Who's more optimistic or pessimistic?
  • Who's more academic, more likely to come in with research or hard facts?
  • Who is more opinionated, more likely to have a hot take?
  • Is one of you more the brains, one more the heart?
  • Who's the energetic half? Who's the grounded, calming presence?
  • Who's the easy laugh? Who's more dry in their humour?
  • Who's the cheerleader? Who plays the devil's advocate?
  • Is one of you an expert on a certain subject, and the other asks questions about it?

Conversation tips

  • Don't be afraid to pipe up! Small responses like laughs, mmhms, or gasps go a long way to make the audience feel like they're in the room with you.
  • Moments of talking over each other are ok — but only if they're short and few. Too much simultaneous talking feels messy.
  • Ask questions. This will keep the conversation going and you'll be acting as a stand-in for the audience.
  • Clock how much you're talking compared to your co-host. Co-hosting is a game of ping-pong — it needs two active, aware participants who are listening well.
  • Get used to listening to yourself. Listen back to having real conversations with your co-host — it's different than performing or coaching.

Chapter 12

Producing a Great Podcast

The producer's role in getting a great host performance is about far more than pressing record.

The producer's role

  • Guiding a performer, overtly and covertly
  • Advocate on behalf of both the audience and the performer
  • Be supportive and collaborative
  • Identify a host's superpowers and minimize their vulnerabilities

"Listeners don't know what it is like to be behind the mic, but their opinion is the most important one to me. As a producer, you know the material, you're there to make me sound smart, and you're invested in the success of the show. Who better to give feedback?"

— Matt Galloway, CBC

Questions to ask during a VT session

  • Does the host have energy in their read?
  • Do they sound like they are connecting the words to the listener?
  • Do they end every sentence with the same tone?
  • Are they carrying their energy past the sentence, or dropping before the end?
  • Are they linking sentences too quickly or too slowly?
  • Are they dropping important words? Are verbal tics getting in the way?

Techniques

→You're in a conversation with the host, even though you're not speaking. Face them and engage with reaction.
→Be a cheerleader. First comment is always supportive.
→Know your direction before you give it. Critique with positive action.
→If it's not going well, take a break and regroup.
→Play with the best way to get a natural performance. Try getting the host to deliver a section with their eyes off the script, as they remember it — some hosts need to find it in their own words.
→Leave small surprises in scripts for the hosts — secrets make for fun, live tape. If a guest has a shocking story, prompt the host to ask a question that triggers it, but don't explain it all. That way the host has an authentic, fresh reaction along with the audience.
